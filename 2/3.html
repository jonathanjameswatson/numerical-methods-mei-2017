False position (an application of linear interpolation)

Linear interpolation can be used to approximate a function as a straight line with two coordinates. THe root of this straight line can be used as an approximation to the root of the actual function.

$$\text{One root of } f(x) = x^6 + 4x - 3 \text{ is } \alpha \text{.}$$

$$\text{Find } \alpha \text{ where } 0 < \alpha < 1 \text{.}$$

The root of the linear interpolation between \(a\) and \(b\) is known as \(c\).

To find \(c\), similar triangles can be drawn.

\(f(a)\) is negative so that both heights are positive.

$$\text{The two triangles are similar so } \frac{f(b)}{-f(a)} = \frac{b - c}{c - a}$$

$$f(b)(c - a) = -f(a)(b - c)$$

$$cf(b) - af(b) = cf(a) - bf(a)$$

$$cf(b) - cf(a) = af(b) - bf(a)$$

$$c(f(b) - f(a)) = af(b) - bf(a)$$

$$c = \frac{af(b) - bf(a)}{f(b) - f(a)}$$

Using this formula, \(c\) can be found like in the bisection method. However, the approximation to \(c\) is likely to be more accurate.

A table can be made to demonstrate this process.

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline a & f(a) & b & f(b) & c & f(c) \\ \hline 0 & -3 & 1 & 2 & 0.6 & -0.55334 \\ \hline 0.6 & -0.55334 & 1 & 2 & 0.68669 & -0.14841 \\ \hline 0.68669 & -0.14841 & 1 & 2 & 0.70833 & -0.04038 \\ \hline \end{array}$$

In the above table, \(c\) converges to a value much faster than in the bisection method.
